Case Study · 02

Kindergarten Achievement Analysis

Predicting reading and math scores for kindergarten students using machine learning, and identifying the classroom conditions and socioeconomic factors that drive early achievement.

Context

Academic · Group Project

Role

Modelling, EDA & Feature Analysis

Stack

Python · LightGBM · XGBoost · SHAP · statsmodels

Dataset

Project STAR - 5,060 kindergarten students

01 Business Problem

What classroom conditions shape early learning?

Australia is in the midst of major early-childhood education reforms - raising questions about how class size, teacher quality, and socioeconomic factors affect student outcomes. The landmark Project STAR experiment in Tennessee randomly assigned over 11,000 kindergarten students to small classes, regular classes, or regular classes with a teacher's aide. Because assignment was random within schools, causal inferences about class size are credible.

Chetty et al. (2011) extended this work by linking STAR records to U.S. tax data, finding that kindergarten classroom quality predicts higher earnings, college attendance, and better neighbourhood outcomes in adulthood - making this one of the most consequential early education datasets in existence.

Objective: Build predictive models for reading and math scores using classroom and student covariates, identify the most important drivers of performance, and minimise combined out-of-sample MSE on a withheld test set.

Multicollinearity check (VIF): school_id and schooldistrict_id had VIF values of 68.1 and 66.9 respectively - extreme collinearity. schooldistrict_id was dropped; school_id was retained as it carries the finer-grained school-level variation relevant to the Project STAR experimental design.

Outlier removal: IQR method applied to score_read, score_math, and experience. This removed a small number of extreme values that would have distorted model training without representing realistic student performance.

03 Modelling

Five models. Two scores. One combined objective.

Separate models were trained for reading and math scores. The combined evaluation metric is MSEo = (MSEread + MSEmath) / 2, which is what the assignment competition leaderboard uses. All models used 80/20 train-test split with random state 42, and the same preprocessing pipeline (StandardScaler for numeric, OneHotEncoder drop='first' for categorical).

XGBoost and LightGBM converged to near-identical MSEₒ. XGBoost was selected as the final model for achieving the lowest combined MSE of 990.33, marginally beating LightGBM's 990.80. The tree-based models outperformed linear methods substantially - confirming non-linear interactions between socioeconomic and classroom variables.

Key modelling insight: The reading and math score predictions behave differently. Reading is more predictable (lower MSE) while math shows higher variance - consistent with the research literature suggesting math achievement is more sensitive to classroom-level factors that aren't fully captured in cross-sectional data.

04 Hyperparameter Tuning

GridSearchCV across 72 parameter combinations

06 Key Findings

What this means for education policy

01

SES is the dominant driver

Free-lunch eligibility - a proxy for poverty - is the strongest observable predictor after school identity. Students from lower-SES households score ~17 points lower in reading on average.

02

Small classes do help

Small class assignment lifts reading by ~5.5 points and math by ~8 points on average - a meaningful effect that SHAP confirms is real, though smaller than the SES gap.

03

Teacher experience matters

Experience ranks third in SHAP importance for reading and second for math. More experienced teachers produce measurably better outcomes, independent of class type.

04

Inner-city gap is real

Inner-city school students average 11.1 points below suburban peers in reading - a gap that persists after controlling for individual characteristics.

05

School-level effects dominate

School ID has by far the highest SHAP importance, absorbing unobserved quality variation. This aligns with Chetty et al.'s finding that classroom quality predicts adult outcomes decades later.

06

Tree models beat linear

XGBoost and LightGBM substantially outperformed OLS and regularised models (MSEₒ ~990 vs ~1,050+), confirming non-linear and interaction effects in the data.

07 Limitations & Reflections

What we'd do differently

  • Cross-sectional data only. Models predict a snapshot - they can't capture learning trajectories or the compounding effects of classroom quality over multiple years that Chetty et al. document in the longitudinal data.
  • SES measured by a single binary proxy. Free-lunch eligibility is a rough measure of socioeconomic status. Household income, parental education, and neighbourhood quality would likely improve model fit and reveal more nuanced effects.
  • School ID as a black box. While school_id dominates SHAP, it absorbs all unobserved school-level variation without explanation. Ideally, school-level features (per-pupil funding, teacher turnover, class sizes) would replace it.
  • Random assignment helps - but isn't perfect. Within-school randomisation removes neighbourhood confounders, but can't account for parent lobbying to change class assignments, or mid-year transfers that dilute the treatment effect.
